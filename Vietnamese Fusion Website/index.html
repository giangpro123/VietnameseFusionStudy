Key findings

1.

Why fusion music exist.

Vietnamese traditional music genres like quan họ folk sóng, nhã nhạc court music, ca trù, or đơn ca tài tử has been losing young listeners over the year.  UNESCO recognises these forms of music as Intangible Cultural Heritage to keep guard of the genres. 

Fusion music is a kind of reponse from new Gen Z artists who want to rebuild traditional elements but in a modern production scene.

Some example of Vietnamese traditional genres:

  • Quan họ Bắc Ninh - folk songs from the Red River Delta, with call-and-response between male and female voices. 
  • Chèo - northern folk theatre, a sung-and-spoken storytelling form of singing.
  • Xẩm - street singing originally performed by blind buskers in northern Vietnam. 
  • Ca trù — sung poetry, historically performed in private chambers for connoisseurs. 
  • Đờn ca tài tử — chamber music from the Mekong Delta in the south.
  • Bolero / dân ca — the broader categories of Vietnamese popular folk song. 

Vietnamese melodies use pentatonic (5-note) scales rather than the 7-note Western scales most pop music sits on. Identity comes from instrumental timbre — the bending tone of the đàn bầu (single-string monochord), the bright zither of the đàn tranh, the airy bamboo flute of the sáo trúc — and from vocal ornamentation (slides, vibrato, grace notes). Western pop is built around chord progressions; Vietnamese traditional music is built around how individual notes are sung and shaped.

2.

Three categories of fusion

The nine tracks fall into three groups based on how dominant the traditional element was in the music. 

Vocal-focused
2 of 9 tracks
Traditional elements dominate while modern element act as backing/support for the music.  
Balanced
5 of 9 tracks
Balance between both traditional and modern elements.
Electronic-focused
2 of 9 tracks
Music itself is almost fully modern while traditional elements appear through lyrics, MV, or short references. 
3.

Singing carries the tradition

Across the seven tracks where traditional elements appear in the music itself, the modern genre varied widely (trap, EDM, reggaeton, disco, ballad). In each case, the Vietnamese element came through the vocal style: slides, call-and-response, melisma and many other ornamentation inherited from quan họ, chèo, xẩm, ca trù, and Vietnamese bolero. The sound, genre, and arrangement can change but the vocal always stay traditional.

4.

Two producers, six tracks.

Masew and DTAP together produced six of the nine tracks studied: Bắc Bling, Thị Mầu, Cưỡi Ngựa Về Trời, See Tình, Người Ơi Người Ở Đừng Về, and Bóng Phù Hoa. They arrived to fusion music after building their name in the music world

Masew is a Vietnamese producer who broke through in the EDM scene with Túy Âm in 2017, and started experimenting with traditional Vietnamese instruments into his work later, leading to Thị Mầu, then Bắc Bling.

DTAP are a three-person production trio from Ho Chi Minh City. They broke through with Để Mị Nói Cho Mà Nghe for Hoàng Thùy Linh in 2019, and have since produced See Tình (2022) and Bóng Phù Hoa (2023)

This concentration partly explains why these tracks share a recognisable sound despite belonging to very different genres.

5.

What listeners said

86% agreed fusion is a valuable direction for Vietnamese music. 83% said listening to fusion makes them feel more connected to their cultural roots. 75% said they are proud to be Vietnamese

Only 42% strongly agreed they actively learn or preserve traditional music themselves. The pattern suggests respondents are more willing to listen than to actively preserve.

Survey Results

An online survey held between 14-23 March 2026. 194 Respondents responses were collected. 

Part one

Who answered

Fig. 1 Age

Mostly 18–24.

72.7% of respondents were 18–24. The project is concerned with Gen Z engagement, not the wider population so this is the perfect sample for data collection.

Fig. 2 Gender

Mostly Female

71.6% female, 26.8% male, 1.5% other / prefer not to say. This likely happened because of the distribution channels (university, Facebook groups).

Fig. 3  Musical training

Majority does not play

83% play no instrument. 12.9% play a Western instrument (guitar, piano, violin). 4.1% play a traditional Vietnamese instrument. The sample is of people who are generally not musician but how they feel about fusion music. 

Part two

How they first heard traditional music

Fig. 4 First exposure

Through family/social media

38.1% first encountered traditional music through family. 26.8% through social media (YouTube, TikTok, Facebook Reels). Cultural events accounted for 12.9%, while school has less exposure at 10.8%.

Fig. 5 Familiarity

Decent familiarity with traditional music

The largest group (40.2%) describe themselves as "somewhat familiar" with traditional genres. They have heard of quan họ, chèo, ca trù, xẩm but have not explored them in depth. Only 16.5% can confidently distinguish between specific traditions.

Fig. 6 Frequency

Occasionally, but not often

46.4% listen to traditional music a few times a month. 31.4% only a few times a year. 8.2% listen daily or several times a week.

Part three

Which fusion works

Fig. 7 Acceptance by genre

Pop highest. Rock lowest.

75% rated pop/ballad fusion positively (4 or 5 from 1-5). 67% for hip-hop/rap, 64% for EDM. Rock and jazz scored lowest, with 22% rejecting it actively. 

Fig. 8 Mean acceptance

The results follow mainstream interests

Pop (4.14), EDM (3.86), and hip-hop (3.81) score highest. These are the dominant genres on Vietnamese streaming.
Jazz (3.41) and rock (3.38) score lowest due to being unpopular in Vietnam. 

0/9
tracks in the analysis use rock or jazz as their modern fusion. Audience and producer both agree
Part four

Views on traditional/fusion music

Fig. 9 Attitudes & identity

Fusion as cultural connection.

86% agree fusion is a valuable direction for Vietnamese music. 88% appreciate artists who fuse traditional and modern elements. 83% say listening to fusion makes them feel more connected to their roots.

Only 42% strongly agree they actively learn or preserve traditional music themselves.

Fig. 10 Behavioural intention

Online over outside

Strongest: following and supporting fusion artists online (mean 4.08 / 5).
Weakest: attending a live performance (mean 3.61).

Many respondents would like to support fusion artists online rather than attending a live show in person.

Fig. 11 Music socialisation

Traditional music digitally

76% agreed social media and digital platforms helped them discover and engage with traditional music. 47% said their families regularly listen to traditional music in daily life.
Through this, we can see that family is the most common point of first exposure while digital platforms keep engagement alive.

Vocal-focused

Bóng Phù Hoa

Phương Mỹ Chi x DTAP · 2023

Watch on YouTube ↗
Instrumentation & timbre

Traditional Vietnamese instruments (đàn tranh, sáo trúc) blended with cinematic orchestral pads, soft piano, and string sections. Phương Mỹ Chi's voice carries traditional vocal warmth.

Rhythm

Slow ballad tempo in 4/4. Gentle, flowing rhythm with rubato moments allowing emotional phrasing. Rhythm follows narrative arc of the story rather than normal convention

Vocal ornamentation

Traditional Vietnamese folk vocal ornamentation - slides, sustained vibrato, expressive bends. Classical poetic phrasing ("trâm hoa vàng", "Hoàng Giang") delivered with reverent inflections. Echoes of ca trù melodic contours in sustained notes.

Electronic components

Atmospheric synth pads, reverb-heavy mix, programmed percussion. Production prioritises traditional instruments rather than overpowering them. Cinematic sweeps for emotional climax.

Structure

Intro → Verse 1 → Verse 2 → Chorus → Verse 3 → Bridge → Final Chorus → Outro

Texture

Mostly homophonic with lead vocal supported by orchestral and traditional instrumental backing. Part of the music is quiet in verses for narrative clarity, building to fuller orchestrated chorus. Bridge features instrumental dialogue between traditional and modern instruments.

Cultural note

Inspired by Chuyện Người Con Gái Nam Xương by 16th-century scholar Nguyễn Dữ - the tragic tale of Vũ Nương, falsely accused of infidelity, who drowned herself in the Hoàng Giang river. Lyrics use classical poetic vocabulary to convey female suffering in feudal Vietnam.
